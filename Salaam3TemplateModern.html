What the Classical Scholars Say About Salaam

The Four Schools of Islamic Law (Madhahib)

The Consensus of All Four Schools:
  • Giving salaam is Sunnah Mu'akkadah (a confirmed Sunnah—highly recommended)
  • Returning salaam is Wajib (obligatory) if you are alone
  • Returning salaam is Fard Kifayah (communal obligation) if given to a group—meaning if some people respond, the obligation is fulfilled for all

The Hanafi School

According to Hanafi scholars:

  • It is Sunnah to initiate the greeting
  • It is Wajib (necessary) to respond when alone
  • The greeting should be clear and audible
  • Deliberately withholding a response can be sinful

Ibn Taymiyyah (Hanbali Scholar, 1263-1328 CE)

Ibn Taymiyyah emphasized:

"Salaam is among the symbols of Islam. It fosters love and unity among Muslims. The one who withholds it due to arrogance or disdain has committed a sin against the rights of his brother."

Ibn al-Qayyim (Student of Ibn Taymiyyah)

Ibn al-Qayyim wrote in his works:

"The greeting of salaam is a supplication for safety and peace. When you say 'As-Salamu Alaikum,' you are praying that Allah protects that person from all harm—seen and unseen. This is why it is so beloved to Allah."

Imam Ahmad ibn Hanbal (Founder of Hanbali School, 780-855 CE)

Imam Ahmad stressed:

"The scholars agree that responding to salaam is obligatory. If someone greets you and you deliberately ignore them, you have committed a sin."

In his famous Musnad, Imam Ahmad collected many hadiths emphasizing the importance of spreading salaam in the community.

Abu Hanifah (Hanafi School, 699-767 CE)

Imam Abu Hanifah and his students taught:

"The greeting must be returned in a manner that is at least equal to what was given. If someone says 'As-Salamu Alaikum,' you must reply with 'Wa Alaikum As-Salam' at minimum. It is better to add 'Wa Rahmatullahi Wa Barakatuhu.'"
